Below are well-regarded, typically “easy” 2000–4000 level courses at many US universities that students commonly take to fulfill credit requirements. Course availability and exact numbering at Georgia State University (GSU) can vary each semester, so use this list to search GSU’s catalog for equivalent offerings and confirmed prerequisites.

High-level picks (broad, low workload, useful credits)

Introduction to Philosophy (PHIL 2010-type)
Focus: basic logic, ethics, epistemology, critical thinking.
Why take it: readings and short essays; good grade potential if you engage in discussion and submit assignments on time.
Intro to Psychology (PSY 2010/2100)
Focus: cognitive, developmental, social psychology overview.
Why take it: lecture-based, multiple-choice exams, lots of study aids.
Sociology or Contemporary Social Problems (SOC 2100 / SOC 2010)
Focus: institutions, inequality, societal issues.
Why take it: lecture and short writing; attendance/participation boosts grade.
Public Speaking or Fundamentals of Communication (COMM 1000/2000-level)
Focus: speech preparation, delivery, persuasion.
Why take it: practical, grade largely from a few speeches; useful transferable skill.
Intro to Ethics (PHIL 2100 or similar)
Focus: moral theories, applied ethics.
Why take it: essay-based but manageable; stimulates critical thought.
American Government / Political Science Survey (POLS 1100/2000-level)
Focus: institutions, constitution, policy basics.
Why take it: factual content, predictable exams.
World or Regional History (HIST 2000–4000 survey courses)
Focus: timelines, key events, broad narratives.
Why take it: lecture+exam format, note-driven study.
Introduction to Women’s & Gender Studies or Cultural Studies (WGS / CULP)
Focus: theory plus contemporary examples.
Why take it: essay and discussion oriented; engaging topics.
STEM-lite / quantitative-lite options (if you prefer non-heavy math)

Introductory Statistics for non-majors (STAT 2000-type)
Focus: basic probability, descriptive/inferential stats.
Why take it: practical, computational; plenty of resources for learning.
Environmental Science or Geology Survey (ENVS / GEOG 2000-level)
Focus: ecosystems, human impacts, physical geography.
Why take it: visual labs, short quizzes, project-based assessments.
Applied/skill courses (useful, high pass rates)

Introduction to Film or Film Appreciation (FILM 2000-level)
Focus: cinema history, analysis.
Why take it: film viewings and short response papers.
Introduction to Digital Media or Basic Graphic Design (ART/DMST 2000-level)
Focus: practical software and projects.
Why take it: hands-on assignments; creative and often lenient grading.
Personal Finance or Financial Literacy (BUS/ECON 2000-level)
Focus: budgeting, credit, investing basics.
Why take it: short assignments, real-world utility.
Tips to maximize GPA and minimize workload

Check syllabi before enrolling: grading breakdown, major assignments, and final exam weight determine true workload.
Prefer lecture + low-frequency major assignments: classes with weekly quizzes or regular short assignments are often easier to keep up with than those with a single heavy final.
Look for courses with attendance/participation components if you can attend reliably — small non-academic points add up.
Read recent course reviews: RateMyProfessors, campus Facebook groups, and upperclassmen can identify easy or grade-friendly instructors.
Avoid project-heavy or lab-heavy science courses if you want minimal out-of-class time.
Use Canvas/Blackboard to preview past announcements/assignments when accessible; it reveals actual course pacing.
Balance: pair one easy course with one slightly more challenging to manage workload spikes.
